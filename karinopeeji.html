Minsys (Tentative Name)

Minsys (Tentative Name) is shortened form of “Minsys (Tentative Name) is the Illion-number Naming System”. The part of “M” is derived from the initial letter of the username of Meauk, but the original meaning is lost in the official name, and Minsys (Tentative Name) has resultantly become a recursive acronym.

[2-2] Standard Conway-Guy System

Olivier Miakinen proposed to change "Quinqua-" into "Quin-", based on the fact that Latin name of 15 is quindecim.

The least illion number, which differs mutually in the Original Conway-Guy System and the Standard Conway-Guy System, is the 15-illion:

  • Quinquadecillion if in the Original
  • Quindecillion if in the Standard

[2-3] Extended Conway-Guy System

Before Miakinen proposing the above, Conway and Guy extended the Original Conway-Guy System with Allan Wechsler for n ≥ 1000; “XilliYilliZillion” (say) denotes the (1000000X + 1000Y + Z)th zillion, using “nillion” for the zeroth “zillion” when this is needed as a placeholder.

This extension is easily applicable to the Standard Conway-Guy System; we just use “Quin-” instead of “Quinqua-”.

However, the digit grouping marker, which is spelled “illi”, has no usefulness equivalent to illion numbers themselves. It is because it has no name indicating its position, and this fact denies the potentiality that Conway-Guy System could additionally be extended.

[2-4] Simplified Conway-Guy System

Even after the proposal of Miakinen, the Standard Conway-Guy System still has some illion-number names different from the common; the 16th and 19th illion numbers are known commonly as Sexdecillion and Novemdecillion better than Sedecillion and Novendecillion, which are the names generated in the system.

In addition, some componential form changes are complicated. (cf. what corresponds to three, six, seven and nine, respectively)

There are some extensions of the Standard Conway-Guy System, which are based on the reinterpretation that its components of “Deci-” and “Centi-” are SI prefixes. The typical example is the system by Jonathan Bowers, and there are three critiques as below.

  1. In his system and its extensions by the others, the 1,000,000th illion number is called “Micrillion” because of the SI prefix “Micro-”, but this means small in Greek or 10-6 as one SI prefix. Like this, the reinterpretation on which they are based is the arbitrariness itself of them.
  2. Also, some names exist as synonyms in the identical systems.
  3. In addition, they do not have the natural extensibility in them, and the users of them cannot be away from continuing naming illion numbers arbitrarily.

[3] Concept of Tier - Understanding Illion Numbers Hierarchically

For for more extensions, the introduction of the concept called “tier” is necessary.

Before the explanation of the concept of “tier”, we need to see the definitions of two functions:

  1. F(n) = 106n for the Long Scale
  2. and f(n) = 103n+3 for the Short Scale.

Their own input value “n” must include at least one of the two kinds of terms:

  1. what corresponds to just one integer (any of one to 999,999 if in the Long Scale or any of zero to 999 if in the Short Scale), and this kind of term is only one in the identical input value
  2. and what consists of one integer (Basically the same condition as the above, except that the multiplier is exceptionally allowed to be zero for terms that are zero in the Long Scale) as a multiplier and another function (“F” if in the Long Scale or “f” if in the Short Scale) as a multiplicand, where the nested function is numbered #1 to #999,999 if in the Long Scale or #0 and #999 if in the Short Scale, and the numbering determines one integer corresponding to the first kind of term in the nested function.

The “tier” is the concept for regarding both the integer in the first kind above and the multiplier in the second as being in one group, but the mupltipliers equal to zero are always ignored in both of the scales .

For the numbering of a tier, the group located immediately outside of the outermost function is called Tier 0 or the zeroth tier, and that located immediately inside of the function is called Tier 1 or the first tier. The numbering of a tier is defined recursively through this process.

[5] Explanation of Tier-Visualizers

The tier-visualizers makes three kinds of units:

  1. Fundamental Unit
    • a set of one Latin-origin cardinal-related component and one Greek-origin ordinal component
      (The latter can be called tier-visualizer.)
    • e.g. Tre- + deutero-
      (Tier 0 numerals are included conveniently because they can be found to be Tier 0 as they are.)
  2. Stair-like Unit
    • a set of multiple fundamental units
    • e.g. three billion (three in tier 0 and two in tier 1)
      (The tiers in this unit are ascending-ordered.)
  3. Ruler-like Unit
    • a set of multiple stair-like units
    • e.g. five quattuorprototredeuterillion three quadrillion five
      (= 5 × f(4 × f(3)) + 3 × f(4) + 5, where f(x) means x-illion)
    • (Each of the stair-like units looks ascending-ordered (which is like small scales on a ruler), but the set of the highest tiers of each of the stair-like units looks descending-ordered (which is like big scales on a ruler))
